Methodology

Model specification

The predictive tool uses multiple linear regression to model the average pollution removal (grams/yr) for each London Plane tree based upon it's carbon sequestration (grams/yr), spread (m) and height (m) . The model is shown as:
y = b0 + b1x1 + b2x2 + b3x3
Where:
y = average pollution removal per tree
b0 is the constant term
b1 is the coefficient for the effect of carbon sequestration denoted x1
b2 is the coefficient for the effect of spread denoted x2
b3 is the coefficient for the effect of height denoted x3

With these estimated coefficients, the model results in:
y = -510.9830 -3.56 x1 + 93.1614 x2+ 8.7374 x3
The model explains 89% of the variation in average air pollution removal for a sample size n of 3217 London Plane trees in Camden.

Model selection

The initial pool of predictors consisted of a multitude of predictors selected from the Camden API â€“ they were selected based on the researchersâ€™ assessment of variables that could be relevant in modelling pollution removal. The initial pre-selected variables consisted of: height, spread, diameter (centimeters at breast height), physiological condition, capital asset value, carbon storage (kilograms), gross carbon sequestration (kilograms per year).

They were then filtered twice: first by applying stepwise regression and then by accounting for multicollinearity among the remaining predictors.

In the stepwise regression, the p-value of each predictor had to be below 0.6 for the predictor to be kept in the regression. Physiological condition was the only predictor above this threshold and was therefore dropped. In the multicollinearity test, which was assessed by calculating the Variation Inflation Factor of each remaining predictor, the VIF threshold was of 10. Several predictors were multicollinear among each other: a meter, Amenity Value and Carbon Storage were removed for the final regression to remain with x1, x2 and x3 or carbon sequestration, spread and height.

Prediction

The model predicts future carbon sequestration each year for over a period of five years. The future values of carbon sequestration, spread and height with which the model was applied are assigned based on external research on the London Plane tree:

  • Carbon sequestration increases by 2.5 kilograms per year
  • Spread increases by 0.5 meters per year
  • Height increases by one meter per year
By being linear, the model also assumes the relation between those predictors and the outcome is the same for all trees regardless

Perspectives (maybe change to evaluation?)



This is a simple model that provides an initial understanding of the potential that trees bring to Camden in terms of environmental, social and economic sustainability. The model is reliable in raising such awareness as it satisfies four out of five assumptions of linear regression and has high explanatory power. However, the model cannot predict the exact environmental, social and economic value of trees, as: The quality and meaning of the data is questionable as the Borough of Camden has provided little to no metadata on the meaning, reliability and collection method of each variable. The relation between the predictors and the outcome is dependent on the maturity of the trees which is ignored by the model. The relation is also not linear, but other tested relations did not yield satisfactory results. For more details, contact Sara Moatti or Nicolas Koedjikov.

Conclusion

This project was drafted by a group of students in order to communicate the presence and importance of the trees within Camden Council. Our means are twofolds: vizualisation of the location of "public" trees (trees that are within council-owned property) through a multi-layered map and interpretation of council-provided data on the trees through summary statistics and regression analysis.

All information on this website is open to public access. The data was sourced from the Camden Council Tree Dataset.
