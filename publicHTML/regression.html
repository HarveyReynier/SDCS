Data Analysis

In this section we look further into the data we sourced from Camden Council. We apply some advanced analytics tools in order to get a model reliable enough for prediction. The predicted value is the pollution removal in Camden, specifically by the London Plane type of tree. The end result is a predictive tool for the pollution removal in Camden for the years 2021 to 2025.

Hypothesis

Does pollution removal by the urban forest in Camden change over time? Does it change homogeneously across wards?

Our initial assumption was that there would be an increase in pollution removal, due to the natural growth of trees and the consequential increase in their capacity to remove pollution (the bigger the tree, the bigger the pollution removal). Therefore, we expected the increase to be relatively similar among wards. However, that was not what we found!

Main Findings

The London Plane tree pollution removal in Camden is predicted to increase significantly by an average of 7.7% per year.
By 2025, it will have increased by 45%, compared to 2020, ranging from 15% in Kingâ€™s Cross, the lowest increase, to 70% in West Hampstead, the highest increase.

Overall, it is in the North and the North-West of the borough that there will be the highest increase in the pollution removed by London Planes. This could make the hotspots of air pollution removal disappear, as both North and South would have high air pollution removal values, therefore becoming more similar.

Methodology

Model specification

The predictive tool uses Multiple Linear Regression (MLR) to model the average pollution removal (grams/yr) for each London Plane tree based on its carbon sequestration (grams/yr), spread (m) and height (m). The model is shown as:
y = b0 + b1x1 + b2x2 + b3x3
Where:
y is the predicted average pollution removal per London PLane tree
b0 is the constant term
x1 is carbon sequestration with coefficient b1
x2 is tree spread with coefficient b2
x3 is tree height with coefficient b3

With these estimated coefficients, the model results in:
y = -510.9830 -3.56 x1 + 93.1614 x2+ 8.7374 x3
The model explains 89% of the variation in average air pollution removal for a sample size n of 3217 London Plane trees in Camden.

Model selection

The initial pool of predictors consisted of a multitude of variables based on our assessment of relevant variables in modelling pollution removal. The initial pre-selected variables were: height, spread, Diameter at Breast Height, physiological condition, Capital Asset Value, carbon storage and gross carbon sequestration.
The variables were then filtered twice: first by applying stepwise regression and then by accounting for multicollinearity among the remaining predictors. In the stepwise regression, the p-value of each predictor had to be below 0.6 for the predictor to be kept in the regression. In the multicollinearity test, the Variation Inflation Factor threshold was of 10.
The final remaining variables were therefore carbon sequestration (x1), spread (x2) and height (x3).

Model Outcome

The model predicts future pollution removal each year by the London Plane trees over a period of five years (2020 to 2025). The future values of carbon sequestration(x1), spread (x2) and height (x3) with which the model was applied were assigned based on external research on the London Plane tree where we found that:

  • Carbon sequestration increases by 2.5 kilograms per year
  • Spread increases by 0.5 meters per year
  • Height increases by one meter per year
By being linear, the model also assumes the relation between those predictors and the outcome is the same for all trees.

Evaluation



This is a simple model that provides an initial understanding of the potential that trees bring to Camden in terms of environmental, social and economic sustainability. The model is reliable in raising such awareness as it satisfies four out of five assumptions of linear regression and has high explanatory power. However, the model cannot predict the exact environmental, social and economic value of trees, as: The quality and meaning of the data is questionable as the Borough of Camden has provided little to no metadata on the meaning, reliability and collection method of each variable. The relation between the predictors and the outcome is dependent on the maturity of the trees which is ignored by the model. The relation is also not linear, but other tested relations did not yield satisfactory results, so this was the best method among those we tried.

Conclusion


This tool allows us to see two main things. Firstly, we observe that certain wards in the North and the North-West of Camden borough will witness a higher increase in pollution removal over the coming 5 yearsâ€™ time than the rest of the Borough. This will have an effect of reducing the hotspot effect by bringing the northern and southern wards to closer similarities in pollution removal values. Secondly, we observed that according to our model, the highest coefficient is the one related to tree spread. This means that tree spread is highly correlated to the change in pollution removal by the London Plane; the bigger the London Plane tree, the bigger the change in pollution removal by that tree!
